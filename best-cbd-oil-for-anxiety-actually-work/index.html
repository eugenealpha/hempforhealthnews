The best CBD Oil for Anxiety: How does CBD help anxiety?

As far back as 1993 researchers have been looking at CBD to treat anxiety. A study was done by A. W. Zuardi, R. A. Cosme, F. G. Graeff and F. S. Guimarães, Effects of ipsapirone and cannabidiol on human experimental anxiety which found in human subjects “The results suggest that ipsapirone and CBD have anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) properties in human volunteers submitted to a stressful situation.”

Then in 2011 more research was conducted that found “Relative to placebo, CBD was associated with significantly decreased subjective anxiety”. This study titled Neural basis of anxiolytic effects of cannabidiol (CBD) in generalized social anxiety disorder: a preliminary report  looked at the underlying brain mechanisms to try and understand how CBD works to help with anxiety.

We’re still discovering how CBD works, but the answer to how it helps with anxiety may lie in the Endocannabinoid System or the ECS.

Introducing the Endocannabinoid System

Every mammal on planet Earth has an Endocannabinoid System or ECS. The ECS is a biological system that includes endocannabinoids which are cannabinoids that our bodies produce naturally.

Cannabidiol or CBD interacts with these ECS receptors located throughout the body. The ECS system is known to regulate a number of physiological and cognitive processes in the body including:

  • appetite
  • stress response
  • pain-sensation
  • mood
  • memory
  • contributing to exercise-induced euphoria
  • modulating locomotor activity

It’s not hard to imagine that a system that impacts so many critical body functions should be kept in “balance”.  This balance is often referred to as homeostasis. Many people believe that generalized social anxiety disorder or SAD is related to the ECS being out of balance.

CBD may help to bring the ECS into homeostasis and help to regulate these stress response and mood processes.

The Risks of taking CBD Oil for Anxiety

CBD is considered to be non-toxic even in large amounts. A study in 2011 found CBD to be initially safe.

“Several studies suggest that CBD is non-toxic in non-transformed cells and does not induce changes on food intake, does not induce catalepsy, does not affect physiological parameters (heart rate, blood pressure and body temperature), does not affect gastrointestinal transit and does not alter psychomotor or psychological functions. Also, chronic use and high doses up to 1,500 mg/day of CBD are reportedly well tolerated in humans. Conversely, some studies reported that this cannabinoid can induce some side effects, including inhibition of hepatic drug metabolism, alterations of in vitro cell viability, decreased fertilization capacity, and decreased activities of p-glycoprotein and other drug transporters. Based on recent advances in cannabinoid administration in humans, controlled CBD may be safe in humans and animals. However, further studies are needed to clarify these reported in vitro and in vivo side effects.”

Conclusions

While continuing research into CBD and the role it might play in helping people with anxiety is needed, the current CBD products on the market might be worth trying.
